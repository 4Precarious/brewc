Amounts Owed

The "Amounts Owed" category of your credit score analyzes your use of your available credit. This category considers both your installment loans (e.g. a mortgage or car loan) and your revolving accounts (e.g. credit cards, lines of credit). Generally speaking, your revolving credit usage is more heavily weighted (positively and negatively) in the score.

Revolving Utilization Ratio Importance

This references the balances you owe on revolving accounts compared to the limits on those revolving accounts. If you have a $1,000 limit credit card and you owe $500, your revolving utilization ratio is 50%. Add up the balances on all of your revolving accounts and divide by the sum of the limits on all your revolving accounts and that's your overall revolving utilization ratio. Lower revolving utilization ratios are considered favorable by a FICO® Score. Remember that FICO® Scores Consider how you use your revolving credit in several ways: 1) your overall revolving utilization ratio (discussed above) and 2) your utilization ratio on specific types or number of accounts (discussed below).

Credit Limit Increase Instructions

When consumers carry a high debt-to-credit-limit ratio (higher ratios – such as 50% or greater – are considered riskier by FICO® Score as compared to lower ratios) it's possible to make immediate score improvement by increasing your credit limit on your current credit cards, thus lowering your overall debt-to-credit-limit ratio.

For example: if you have a $900 balance on one credit card with a $1,000 credit limit, that particular card would have a 90% revolving utilization ratio. However, if your credit card provider were to increase your credit limit to $2,000, you would only be at 45%. This may positively impact the score.

To request a credit limit increase, contact your credit card provider. If your card is in good standing, you may be able to increase your credit limit immediately. Consumers can either notify the customer service department that you would like to increase your credit limit, or you may visit the credit card provider's website, log in, and navigate to the section entitled "Request A Credit Limit Increase."

While these requests to increase your credit limit generally show up as a soft inquiry (AMAccount Monitoring, AR - Account Review, Update) or an inquiry that is not damaging to your credit score, it's always wise to verify with the credit card provider that a hard inquiry, or an inquiry that could negatively affect your score, will not appear on your credit report.
